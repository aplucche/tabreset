The tab problem

Tabs become a to-do list you never asked for. Each one is something you meant to read or come back to. Closing them feels like giving up. So they pile up until your browser slows down and finding anything becomes impossible.

TabReset gives you permission to close tabs by making sure you won't lose them. One click: summarized, saved, closed.

How it works

  1. 1
    Pick a file

    Choose where to save your tab archive. It's a Markdown file. Open it in any text editor, Obsidian, or Notion.

  2. 2
    Click "Summarize & Reset"

    TabReset reads each tab, generates a one-line summary, and appends it to your file with the title, link, and timestamp.

  3. 3
    Tabs close, archive grows

    Your browser is clean. Your archive is searchable. When you need that article from three weeks ago, grep for it.

Your tabs stay on your machine

The AI runs locally in your browser using a small language model (LFM2-350M). No servers, no accounts. Nothing leaves your computer.

The model downloads once (about 700MB), then everything happens offline. Your tab contents are never transmitted anywhere.

Local processing

Summarization happens in-browser via WebAssembly. The model runs on your CPU.

Your files, your location

You pick where to save. Desktop, Dropbox, a git repo. Wherever works.

Append-only

TabReset only adds to your file. It never reads, deletes, or modifies existing content.

Under the hood

TabReset prioritizes speed. Before running the LLM, it checks for meta descriptions and Open Graph tags. If a page already has a good summary, it uses that instead. For pages without metadata, it extracts article content and runs it through the model.

Built on Transformers.js and the Chrome File System Access API. The extension is open source.
